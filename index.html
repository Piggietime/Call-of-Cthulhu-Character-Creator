Step 1 — Choose Your Era

Welcome to Call of Cthulhu. You play an ordinary person — a librarian, a doctor, a soldier — drawn into contact with cosmic horrors beyond human comprehension. Your investigator will likely lose sanity, and may not survive. That's the game.

The era you choose shapes the world your investigator lives in: available skills, occupations, and the overall tone.

1920SThe classic experience. Jazz Age New England, Arkham, and the Miskatonic University. Inspired by Lovecraft's original stories. Most published adventures are set here.
VICTORIAN1890s London. Gaslight, empire, and gothic dread. A world of séances, opium dens, and things that should not be. Different skills, no automobiles, a grittier tone.
MODERNPresent day. Computers, mobile phones, and the internet — but the Mythos doesn't care. Easier to research; harder to explain away.

Step 2 — Investigator Details

Investigators are not heroes. They are ordinary people — architects, nurses, journalists, academics — who stumble into contact with something that was never meant to be known. The more "normal" they are, the more effective the horror when the Mythos touches them.

Fill in the basics: name, age, and physical description. These fields don't affect game mechanics, but they matter enormously at the table. A well-sketched investigator is someone your Keeper can describe vividly and someone you'll feel genuinely worried about losing.

The Personal Description field is especially useful — jot down a line or two about how your investigator carries themselves, what they dress like, a tell or a habit. This is what the Keeper reads aloud when another character meets them for the first time.

Step 3 — Occupation

Your occupation gives you your skill points. Each occupation specifies a formula — usually EDU×4 — and a set of occupation skills. You'll distribute those points among the listed skills in Step 6.

Occupation skills are highlighted in gold on the Skills step. You can spend occupation points only on those skills. Any remaining points go to waste — choose an occupation whose skills match the investigator you want to play.

SKILL POOLThe formula (e.g. EDU×4) is calculated from your final characteristics. Pump EDU if you want more skill points overall.
CREDIT RATINGReflects social standing and wealth. Higher CR means you can afford better equipment and are taken more seriously. The range shown is the allowed minimum and maximum for your occupation.
EXTRA SKILLSMany occupations let you choose N additional skills from a list. These count as occupation skills for point allocation purposes.

Step 4 — Pulp Cthulhu (Optional)

Pulp Cthulhu is an optional variant inspired by the two-fisted heroes of 1930s pulp magazines — Doc Savage, The Shadow, Indiana Jones. Investigators are tougher, more capable, and less likely to die in the first session.

If your Keeper is running standard Call of Cthulhu, leave this step disabled. If they've confirmed Pulp mode, enable it here.

HIT POINTSPulp HP = (CON+SIZ)÷5, doubled from the standard formula. Your investigator is significantly harder to kill.
ARCHETYPEA broad role that defines your investigator's niche. Choose one that matches your concept — it grants bonus skills (100 points to distribute) and restricts or guides talent selection.
TALENTSSpecial abilities that bend the rules in your favour — bonus dice on certain rolls, extra actions, resistance to harm. Most archetypes let you choose 2 freely; hover each talent to read its effect before choosing.

Step 5 — Characteristics

Eight characteristics define your investigator's raw potential. Most are rolled as 3D6×5 (range 15–90); SIZ, INT, and EDU use 2D6+6×5 (range 40–90). Click Roll All for a full random set, or enter numbers manually.

STR / CONStrength and Constitution. Affect Hit Points (CON+SIZ÷10), Build, and Damage Bonus. High CON helps you survive.
SIZ / DEXSize affects HP and Build. Dexterity affects your Dodge skill and acts as a tiebreaker. Lower SIZ with high DEX is a common nimble-investigator build.
APPAppearance. Affects social interactions. Base for Persuade and Charm in some situations. Often overlooked but useful.
INT / EDUThese matter most. EDU×4 is the most common occupation skill formula. INT×2 gives Personal Interest points. Maximise both for a scholar/investigator build.
POWWillpower and psychic resistance. Your starting Sanity = POW×5. High POW means more sanity to lose before going mad.
LUCKRolled separately (3D6×5). Luck is spent during play to improve rolls — it doesn't recover easily, so spend it wisely.

Step 6 — Skills

Skills are percentages. To use a skill, roll 1D100 — roll equal to or under the skill to succeed. Half the skill is a Hard success; one-fifth is an Extreme success. Higher is always better.

You have two point pools to spend: Occupation points (highlighted gold in the skill list) can only go on your occupation's skills. Personal Interest points (INT×2) can go on any skill at all. A few points in Library Use, Spot Hidden, and Psychology will serve almost any investigator well.

CAPNo skill may exceed 90% during character creation. The 99% ceiling exists but is only reached through advancement in play.
SPOT HIDDENThe single most-used skill in the game. Governs noticing clues, ambushes, and things in the dark. Invest here.
LIBRARY USEResearch — finding tomes, newspaper archives, academic records. Almost every adventure requires it at some point.
PSYCHOLOGYDetecting lies and reading emotional states. Surprisingly useful when NPCs are trying to hide something — which they usually are.

Step 7 — Weapons & Equipment

Call of Cthulhu is not a combat game. Fighting is dangerous and often suicidal — the horrors investigators face are rarely killable by conventional weapons. Firearms cause noise, attract authorities, and many monsters simply don't care about bullets.

That said, investigators should be equipped for the situations they're likely to face. A handgun for self-defence, a knife for getting into locked crates, a camera for evidence, a medical kit for the inevitable aftermath.

ERA FITStick to weapons and tools appropriate to your era and Credit Rating. A Victorian investigator won't have a submachine gun; a Modern one won't be carrying a sword as their primary weapon.
SKILL MATCHOnly equip weapons where you have a meaningful skill (40%+). A handgun with Firearms (Handgun) at 20% is a liability in a firefight.
NON-COMBATTorches, rope, first aid kits, and cameras often matter more than weapons. Think about what your investigator would actually carry on their person.
